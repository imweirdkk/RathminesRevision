## Distinguishing between dependant and independent variables 

- Variables we control or allow to vary the the **independent variables**
- Variables that we *do not* control are the **dependent variables**
- Typically we only allow these two to vary and keep everything else that might be relevant constant. These are called controls.

e.g. We are putting out a new product, SynergyConnection, and are considering different price points. Our target demographic is people in their 20s and 30s. We demonstrate the product for four focus groups and tell them the price is €35, €40, €45, and €50. We record the percentage that tell use they would buy the product. Identify:

a) the independent variable:
	the price of the product
b) the dependent variable:
	the percentage of members who say they would buy the product
c) any controls:
	The demographic, how the question is phrased

## Sampling

- **Primary data** is data that we collect ourselves, for example a survey we create of performing an experiment
- **Secondary data** is data collected by others and published for us to use, such as a journal article or the census

## Describing methods of primary data collection
- Some data is given by participants, such as surveys/questionnaires, interviews and focus groups
- Some data is collected by researched from observations or experiment. This can include modern techniques such as web scraping
- Any data that is pre-existing but is not yet collected and compiled is primary data, such as letters, court records and interview transcriptions

## Describing various sources of secondary data
- Recalling that we collect secondary data indirectly from other published sources. The main sources that are typically used are:
	- Government-published data -- Censuses, reports on cso.ie, etc...
	- Published research -- books, journal articles...
	- Published commercial data -- newspaper/magazines/web articles, databases
	- Historical documents -- collections of letters, almanacs, etc...
